AI-Powered Salmon Monitoring

The Problem

The Secwépemc Fisheries Commission monitors salmon populations in the Deadman Watershed using a resistivity counter and cameras, but the current system is error-prone and requires significant manual labour to validate events. Accurate, timely counts are difficult to deliver, leaving harvest and conservation decisions without the numbers they need until months after the season ends.

The Approach

Build an AI system that detects, tracks, and counts salmon directly from camera footage. The system detects salmon in each frame, a tracking layer locks their identities across the video, and a robust counting algorithm increments directional counts while avoiding double-counting. Outputs are stored in a database accessible via a password-protected portal, where partners can download results as CSV files that fit their existing workflows.

The Impact

By automating detection and counting, the system significantly reduces the manual analysis and validation burden on the team — freeing personnel to focus on higher-value work. Instead of waiting for counts after the season ends, partners can now share near real-time estimates with community members and act on them when it matters most.

Results
  • ~98% mAP50 across 400 training epochs — the model correctly locates and identifies fish in nearly every frame it sees
  • 97% precision — 97 out of 100 detections are correct
  • 99% recall — out of every 100 real fish that swim through, the model misses only 1
  • 98% F1 — accurate and consistent across both precision and recall
  • Upstream counts within 2% of SFC manually verified numbers
  • Net counts within 1% of SFC manually verified numbers
  • Presented at the BC PARF Conference, May 2026
